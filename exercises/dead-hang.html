Dead Hang

Grip Forearms Lats Shoulders (decompression) Thoracic Spine

The Dead Hang is exactly what it sounds like — hanging from a bar with straight arms and relaxed shoulders. Despite its simplicity, it's one of the most effective exercises for shoulder health, spinal decompression, and grip strength. Hanging creates traction through the spine, opens the shoulders, and strengthens grip endurance. Dr. John Kirsch's research shows that regular hanging can help resolve shoulder impingement and rotator cuff issues. It's also fundamental preparation for pull-ups and any overhead activity.

Form Cues

  1. Grip the bar with an overhand grip, hands shoulder-width apart
  2. Step off the box or bench and let your body hang freely
  3. Relax your shoulders — let them rise toward your ears initially
  4. Keep arms fully straight — no bending at the elbows
  5. Breathe normally — don't hold your breath
  6. Let your spine decompress — feel the traction
  7. Gently engage your shoulders (pull them slightly down) for an active hang variation
  8. Step back onto the box to dismount — don't just drop

Common Mistakes

  • Bending the elbows (keep arms fully straight for a true dead hang)
  • Holding breath — breathe normally throughout
  • Gripping too tightly — moderate grip force is sufficient
  • Swinging or kipping — stay still
  • Jumping down from height (step down to protect joints)

Progressions & Regressions

Easier
Use a lower bar with feet touching ground for partial bodyweight
Harder
Single-arm hang, add weight with a dip belt, active hang with scapular engagement
